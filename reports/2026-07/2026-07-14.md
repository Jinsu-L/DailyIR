- **Topic**: FAIR GraphRAG – a fully FAIR‑compliant retrieval‑augmented generation framework for biomedical knowledge graphs
- **Aim**: Automate the construction of a FAIR‑compliant knowledge graph of FAIR Digital Objects (FDOs) and integrate it into a GraphRAG pipeline to provide accurate, explainable, provenance‑rich LLM responses for domain‑specific queries
- **Rationale**: LLMs frequently hallucinate domain facts; existing RAG systems ignore relational structure and lack FAIR compliance, leading to poor retrieval quality and unsafe AI recommendations in medicine. A FAIR‑compliant KG with ontology alignment and persistent identifiers is needed to ensure findability, accessibility, interoperability, and reusability at the sub‑graph level
- **Ground**: FAIR GraphRAG introduces a six‑stage LLM‑driven pipeline: 1) schema definition via few‑shot prompting; 2) schema‑guided information extraction; 3‑6) FAIRification (PIDs, metadata, ontology alignment, relationship extraction, Neo4j loading). Each graph node is an FDO with bit sequence, metadata, operations, and PID. The KG is integrated into GraphRAG for query‑driven subgraph retrieval, prompt augmentation, and explainable generation that includes Cypher queries, retrieved FDOs, and provenance. Evaluation on the GSE280797 gastroenterology RNA‑seq dataset shows that GPT‑4o‑mini FAIR‑aware achieves 92.86% accuracy/coverage with 100% explainability, outperforming non‑FAIR baselines and highlighting the importance of LLM choice and prompt engineering
- **Experiment**: The study compared FAIR‑aware versus baseline (non‑FAIR) GraphRAG across GPT‑4o‑mini and Llama‑3.3‑70B models. Metrics included overall accuracy, coverage, and explainability (presence of Cypher query, retrieved nodes, PID, metadata). Results: GPT‑4o‑mini FAIR‑aware: 92.86% accuracy/coverage, 100% explainability; Llama‑3.3‑70B FAIR‑aware: 33.33% accuracy, 35.71% coverage, 100% explainability; GPT‑4o‑mini baseline: 42.86% accuracy/coverage, 66.66% explainability; Llama‑3.3‑70B baseline: 19.05% accuracy/coverage, 66.66% explainability. The FAIR KG improved retrieval recall, reduced hallucinations, and enabled confident, provenance‑rich answers
- **Takeaway**: Embedding FAIR principles into a knowledge‑graph RAG pipeline yields substantial gains in accuracy, coverage, and explainability for biomedical queries. FAIR GraphRAG demonstrates domain‑agnostic scalability, rapid expansion via automated LLM pipelines, and supports privacy‑preserving deployment. Future work should focus on prompt optimization, FDO versioning, RDF interoperability, and long‑term maintenance to sustain FAIR compliance

#### Abstract
> Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) addresses the limitations of Large Language Models (LLMs) when providing responses to domain-specific questions. Graph-based RAG approaches, such as GraphRAG, enhance retrieval by capturing semantic relationships within knowledge graphs (KGs). While the FAIR principles (Findability, Accessibility, Interoperability, and Reusability) are becoming prevalent for scientific data management, especially in complex domains such as medicine, existing RAG approaches lack a structured FAIRification of the underlying knowledge resources. This lack limits their potential for FAIR information retrieval in these domains. To address this gap, we introduce FAIR GraphRAG, a novel framework that integrates FAIR Digital Objects (FDOs) as the fundamental units of a graph-based retrieval system. Each graph node represents an FDO that incorporates core data, metadata, persistent identifiers, and semantic links. We leverage LLMs to support schema construction and automated extraction of content and metadata from data sources. The framework was co-designed by physicians and computer scientists to ensure technical and clinical relevance. We apply FAIR GraphRAG to a biomedical dataset in gastroenterology, demonstrating its applicability to RNA-sequencing data. Beyond ensuring adherence to the FAIR principles, FAIR GraphRAG significantly improves question answering accuracy, coverage, and explainability, particularly for complex queries involving metadata and ontology links. This work shows the feasibility of combining FAIR data practices with graph-based retrieval techniques. We see potential for applying our approach to other specialized fields such as education and business.

#### Abstract

Despite their efficacy, these methods face two primary limitations: (1) they rely on a single collaborative signal for MLLM fine-tuning, failing to exploit the heterogeneous signals essential for multitask ranking; and (2) they treat multimodal representations as regular item features in ranking models, underutilizing their latent potential for user behavior modeling. #### Abstract

PG has been deployed on Taobao Search with statistically significant online A/B uplifts of +0.47% in transaction count and +0.51% in GMV, and has been applied across multiple Taobao search and recommendation teams as the iteration framework for generative retrieval.

#### Abstract
> Researchers organize the papers they collect into personal folder hierarchies in reference managers, and route each new paper into the folder where it belongs. #### Abstract
> Offline evaluation is the standard methodology for comparing top-N recommender systems, yet it relies on incomplete relevance information. In most benchmark datasets, only a small subset of user--item preferences is observed, and unjudged items are commonly treated as non-relevant. This missing-as-negative assumption can bias evaluation, penalize plausible recommendations with no recorded feedback, and favour algorithms that concentrate on popular or highly exposed items. We propose an LLM-based framework to expand relevance judgements for offline recommender evaluation. Our approach uses large language models in two complementary roles. First, a preference induction stage summarizes each user's historical interactions into a textual profile that captures their tastes and interests. The resulting enriched judgements provide additional relevance evidence for previously unobserved user--item pairs, enabling ranking metrics to be computed on a more complete basis. Experimental results show that this approach is a promising strategy for improving the robustness of offline top-N evaluation and mitigating the popularity-sensitive distortions caused by sparse feedback.
