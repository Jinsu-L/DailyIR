### 1. Retrieval as Generation: A Unified Framework with Self-Triggered Information Planning

- **LLM Score**: 9
- **Keyword Score**: 7
- **Authors**: Bo Li, Mingda Wang, Gexiang Fang, Shikun Zhang, Wei Ye
- **URL**: <http://arxiv.org/abs/2604.11407v1>

The proposed GRIP framework tightly couples retrieval and reasoning, supporting dynamic multi-step inference, making it highly relevant to your research in information retrieval and natural language processing.

#### T.A.R.G.E.T. Summary (from Full Text)
- **Topic**: GRIP: Generation‑guided Retrieval with Information Planning
- **Aim**: Develop a lightweight, end‑to‑end retrieval‑augmented generation framework that learns to plan when, how, and how many times to query an external retriever by emitting control tokens as part of the autoregressive output of a large language model, thereby eliminating external controllers and achieving state‑of‑the‑art performance on large‑scale open‑domain/multi‑hop QA benchmarks with an 8‑B parameter backbone.
- **Rationale**: Traditional retrieval‑augmented generation relies on external confidence thresholds, multi‑stage pipelines, or separate controllers to decide when to retrieve, which adds complexity and latency. By treating retrieval as another token to be generated, GRIP enables the model to self‑trigger evidence acquisition, reformulation, and termination within a single decoding trajectory, providing fine‑grained, dynamic control and reducing inference overhead.
- **Ground**: Core idea: token‑controlled retrieval and self‑triggered information planning; token‑level control interface with [RETRIEVE], [INTERMEDIARY], [ANSWER], [SOLVED]; self‑triggered planning loop that iteratively decides to retrieve or answer; training paradigm with structured supervision (four answerability types α, β, γ, θ) and reinforcement fine‑tuning using rule‑based rewards (answer fidelity + control accuracy) optimized with DAPO; retrieval budget (default 3 rounds) and efficiency gains (22% reduction in retrieval count); experimental setup covering HotpotQA, PopQA, Natural Questions, WebQuestions, TriviaQA with LLaMA‑3 8B‑Instruct backbone, 8192‑token truncation, supervised fine‑tuning (8 epochs, lr 1e‑6) and RL fine‑tuning (5k samples, lr 1e‑7); baselines include training‑free prompting, dynamic retrieval models (FLARE, DRAGIN, ETC), and training‑based RAG variants; results show state‑of‑the‑art EM/ROUGE/F1 across benchmarks and efficient inference.
- **Experiment**: Datasets: HotpotQA, PopQA, Natural Questions, WebQuestions, TriviaQA (official splits). Metrics: Exact Match, ROUGE‑1/2/L, F1, Avg.Score (mean of all metrics), CoverEM. Corpus: Wikipedia full dump; BM25 top‑3 passages per query. Model: LLaMA‑3 8B‑Instruct. Training: 40k supervised + 5k RL instances, 8 epochs, batch 4 per GPU (32 GPUs), AdamW lr 1e‑6, cosine scheduler. RL: DAPO with BLEU‑style answer reward + 0.5 per correct control token, 5k samples, lr 1e‑7. Baselines: Direct prompting, one‑shot retrieval, GPT‑3.5 Turbo, GPT‑4o, FLARE, DRAGIN, ETC, SFT‑RAG, Self‑RAG, INFO‑RAG, RobustRAG. Results: GRIP achieves top EM/ROUGE/F1 on all five benchmarks, reduces average retrieval count from 1.60 to 1.24 (≈22% reduction) while maintaining accuracy, and outperforms all baselines in composite Avg.Score.
- **Takeaway**: Embedding retrieval decisions as control tokens within the LLM’s autoregressive output enables a unified, self‑triggered planning loop that eliminates external controllers, reduces retrieval calls, and delivers state‑of‑the‑art performance on diverse QA tasks with a lightweight 8‑B model. GRIP demonstrates that fine‑grained token‑level control is a powerful paradigm for efficient retrieval‑augmented generation.

#### Abstract
> We revisit retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) by embedding retrieval control directly into generation. Instead of treating retrieval as an external intervention, we express retrieval decisions within token-level decoding, enabling end-to-end coordination without additional controllers or classifiers. Under the paradigm of Retrieval as Generation, we propose \textbf{GRIP} (\textbf{G}eneration-guided \textbf{R}etrieval with \textbf{I}nformation \textbf{P}lanning), a unified framework in which the model regulates retrieval behavior through control-token emission. Central to GRIP is \textit{Self-Triggered Information Planning}, which allows the model to decide when to retrieve, how to reformulate queries, and when to terminate, all within a single autoregressive trajectory. This design tightly couples retrieval and reasoning and supports dynamic multi-step inference with on-the-fly evidence integration. To supervise these behaviors, we construct a structured training set covering answerable, partially answerable, and multi-hop queries, each aligned with specific token patterns. Experiments on five QA benchmarks show that GRIP surpasses strong RAG baselines and is competitive with GPT-4o while using substantially fewer parameters.

- **Reason**: This paper is highly relevant to your research interests in Information Retrieval, particularly in the context of neural retrievers and dense retrieval. #### T.A.R.G.E.T. Summary (from Full Text)
- **Topic**: Answer-Centric Hard‑Negative Refinement (ARHN) for dense retrieval and retrieval‑augmented generation
- **Aim**: Provide a unified, LLM‑driven framework that cleans hard‑negative training data by extracting answer snippets and listwise ranking them, thereby stabilising contrastive learning and improving retrieval quality.
- **Rationale**: False negatives (passages containing the answer but labelled negative) and ambiguous negatives (partially answering queries) corrupt the embedding space and degrade downstream QA/RAG performance. Unlabeled data is often assumed negative, amplifying these issues. Cleaning hard negatives with explicit answer evidence mitigates noise and yields more reliable contrastive signals.
- **Ground**: ARHN operates in two stages: 1) an open‑source LLM (Qwen3) extracts a verbatim answer snippet (1–3 sentences) for each query–passage pair, outputting NO_ANSWER if none found; 2) a second LLM (RankLLM) listwise ranks all snippets per query according to a hierarchy (correctness, factual consistency, partial correctness, topical relevance, contradiction, NO_ANSWER). Relabeling rules promote passages ranked above the original positive to new positives, filter those ranked below but containing an answer, and keep NO_ANSWER passages as hard negatives. Training uses InfoNCE with 1 positive + 7 hard negatives + in‑batch negatives on E5‑base and LG‑ANNA‑Embedding (Mistral‑7B) with LoRA. Evaluation on the BEIR benchmark (16 open‑domain datasets, 7 OOD datasets) uses nDCG@10. Baselines include No Refinement, TopK‑PercPos, and RLHN.
- **Experiment**: Variants: (i) Relabeling only, (ii) Filtering only, (iii) Combined (R+F). Results: For E5‑base, ARHN (R+F) raises avg nDCG@10 from 0.508 to 0.521 (+1.3) and OOD from 0.425 to 0.446 (+0.8). For LG‑ANNA‑Embedding, ARHN (R+F) raises avg nDCG@10 from 0.546 to 0.554 (+0.8) and OOD from 0.475 to 0.475 (no change). In‑batch negatives and 16‑GPU inference on A100 were used.
- **Takeaway**: ARHN consistently outperforms all baselines across models and datasets by effectively cleaning hard negatives through LLM‑driven answer extraction and ranking, leading to measurable gains in dense retrieval and RAG performance. The two‑stage LLM refinement is a practical, open‑source solution for stabilising contrastive training in open‑domain QA systems.

#### Abstract
> Neural retrievers are often trained on large-scale triplet data comprising a query, a positive passage, and a set of hard negatives. In practice, hard-negative mining can introduce false negatives and other ambiguous negatives, including passages that are relevant or contain partial answers to the query. Such label noise yields inconsistent supervision and can degrade retrieval effectiveness.
  We propose ARHN (Answer-centric Relabeling of Hard Negatives), a two-stage framework that leverages open-source LLMs to refine hard negative samples using answer-centric relevance signals. In the first stage, for each query-passage pair, ARHN prompts the LLM to generate a passage-grounded answer snippet or to indicate that the passage does not support an answer. In the second stage, ARHN applies an LLM-based listwise ranking over the candidate set to order passages by direct answerability to the query. Passages ranked above the original positive are relabeled to additional positives. Among passages ranked below the positive, ARHN excludes any that contain an answer snippet from the negative set to avoid ambiguous supervision.
  We evaluated ARHN on the BEIR benchmark under three configurations: relabeling only, filtering only, and their combination. Across datasets, the combined strategy consistently improves over either step in isolation, indicating that jointly relabeling false negatives and filtering ambiguous negatives yields cleaner supervision for training neural retrieval models. By relying strictly on open-source models, ARHN establishes a cost-effective and scalable refinement pipeline suitable for large-scale training.

#### Abstract
> Generative listwise reranking leverages global context for superior retrieval but is plagued by intrinsic position bias, where models exhibit structural sensitivity to input order independent of relevance. To resolve this dilemma, we propose CapCal (Content-Agnostic Probability Calibration), a training-free framework that mechanically decouples positional bias from ranking decisions. Notably, it unlocks the latent potential of lightweight models (e.g., 0.6B), delivering absolute NDCG gains exceeding 10 points and outperforming both permutation-based aggregation and data-augmentation baselines.
