#### T.A.R.G.E.T. Summary (from Full Text)
- **Topic**: jina‑reranker‑v3.5: a 0.6B‑parameter hybrid‑attention listwise reranker
- **Aim**: Provide state‑of‑the‑art ranking quality across diverse retrieval benchmarks while enabling fast, GPU‑efficient listwise inference via a hybrid local‑global attention schedule and a lightweight interaction pattern.
- **Rationale**: Long‑range cross‑document comparison is essential for listwise reranking but full‑global attention is costly. A hybrid 3L2G schedule (3 local sliding‑window layers, 2 global layers, final mandatory global layer) preserves long‑range signals with linear‑window cost, and the LBNL (last‑but‑not‑late) interaction pattern allows the query to attend to all candidates in a single causal sequence, reducing KV‑cache overhead.
- **Ground**: - 28‑layer transformer backbone (≈0.6B params).  - LBNL interaction: query appended to end of sequence of query+candidates.  - Hybrid 3L2G attention: 17 local + 11 global layers, window size 1024, final global layer mandatory for readout.  - Training data: multi‑domain SFT mixture (legal, medical, finance, semi‑structured, multilingual) with hard negatives from BM25, Jina, BGE, GTE, E5, ColBERT, and LLM‑generated queries.  - Three‑stage self‑distillation: (I) full‑attention teacher training, (II) sparse‑attention adaptation (freeze MLP, train attention, then unfreeze), (III) teacher‑guided distillation with multi‑level loss (rank‑level KL, score MSE, state MSE, embed cosine, contrastive chunk similarity, token‑embedding dispersion).  - All stages use FlashAttention‑2, bf16, listwise batches, 30–45 hard negatives, random doc‑ID/position perturbations.
- **Experiment**: - Evaluated on BEIR, AILACasedocs, and other retrieval benchmarks.  - Hybrid 3L2G outperformed 1L1G, 1L2G, 5L1G patterns in accuracy‑efficiency trade‑off.  - Distillation pipeline closed the performance gap to the full‑attention teacher while achieving up to 1.56× faster listwise inference.  - Ablations showed the final global layer is critical for LBNL readout; local final layer degrades ranking.  - Loss ablations confirmed auxiliary objectives (dispersive, dual‑matching, contrastive chunk similarity) improve score calibration and representation alignment.
- **Takeaway**: jina‑reranker‑v3.5 demonstrates that a carefully designed hybrid attention schedule combined with a last‑but‑not‑late interaction pattern yields a highly efficient, high‑quality listwise reranker.  The three‑stage self‑distillation pipeline successfully transfers teacher performance to the efficient geometry, enabling practical deployment on commodity GPUs.  The model and weights are publicly released under a non‑commercial Hugging Face license.

#### Abstract
> Listwise rerankers are the discriminative core of agentic retrieval pipelines, yet production deployment demands efficiency, domain robustness, and fluency on semi-structured data at the same time. We present jina-reranker-v3.5, a 0.6B-parameter listwise reranker that meets these demands together without sacrificing the cross-document comparison that makes its predecessor jina-reranker-v3 effective. jina-reranker-v3.5 keeps the last-but-not-late (LBNL) interaction of jina-reranker-v3 and reworks it along three axes. It replaces uniform global attention with a hybrid schedule of three sliding-window layers followed by two global layers, pinning the terminal layer to global as LBNL readout requires. It trains on a curated multi-domain mixture that spans legal, medical, financial, multilingual, and structured retrieval. It transfers quality through a three-stage self-distillation recipe in which a full-attention teacher sets an upper bound that a sparse-attention student then recovers under a staged adaptation protocol. jina-reranker-v3.5 reaches 63.20 nDCG@10 on BEIR, matching a 4B model at roughly 7x fewer parameters, and improves over jina-reranker-v3 on MIRACL and RTEB as well. Its largest gains come on semi-structured retrieval, where it lifts nDCG@10 by 9.6 points over jina-reranker-v3 and leads all rerankers of comparable size. The hybrid schedule further cuts listwise inference latency by up to 1.56x. We release the model weights on Hugging Face under a non-commercial license.

#### T.A.R.G.E.T. Summary (from Full Text)
- **Topic**: Matryoshka Hypencoder – Scalable Dense Retrieval via Nested Q‑Nets
- **Aim**: Enable a single dense retrieval model to operate at multiple efficiency‑effectiveness points by generating a family of query‑network (Q‑Net) widths from one HyperNetwork, thus providing flexible trade‑offs between recall and latency.
- **Rationale**: Dense retrieval systems trade recall for latency: bi‑encoders are fast but low‑recall, cross‑encoders are high‑recall but slow. The original Hypencoder introduced a HyperNetwork to generate a lightweight Q‑Net but was limited to a single width, preventing dynamic trade‑offs. Applying the Matryoshka (nested‑prefix) principle allows the HyperNetwork to produce a full parameter set for the widest Q‑Net and truncate it to any desired width in one forward pass, preserving expressivity while enabling efficient CPU inference.
- **Ground**: Background: bi‑encoders (e.g., DPR) lose expressivity; cross‑encoders are computationally heavy; Hypencoder uses a HyperNetwork to generate a lightweight Q‑Net but fixes its width. Matryoshka Hypencoder (MH) introduces Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL) applied to functions: a single HyperNetwork outputs a full parameter set (Theta_{	ext{full}}^q) for the widest Q‑Net. For any target width (m), the HyperNetwork truncates (Theta_{	ext{full}}^q) to (Theta_m^q) and assembles a temporary Q‑Net of hidden width (m). The hyperhead generates weights via query‑dependent attention mechanisms, and a multi‑objective Margin‑MSE loss is applied over all widths. Training is lightweight: freeze the document and query encoders and fine‑tune only the hyperhead.
- **Experiment**: Datasets: MSMARCO Dev, TREC DL 19/20, BEIR suite (TREC COVID, NFCorpus, FiQA, DBPedia, Webis Touché). Metrics: MRR@10 (MSMARCO), nDCG@10 (TREC DL 19/20 and BEIR). Training: 6 days on two A100 GPUs for full Hypencoder; MH uses lightweight fine‑tuning of hyperhead. Results: MH with (d=256) (~0.6 M params) matches or slightly outperforms the full Hypencoder on in‑domain metrics; smallest converging size (128) retains 97 % of full performance. Out‑of‑domain effectiveness preserved with ≥ 50 % of full parameters. Training efficiency: hyperhead fine‑tuning achieves statistically indistinguishable performance from full end‑to‑end training on 7/8 datasets. Inference throughput: 1.6–3.4× increase when using smaller Q‑Nets, enabling CPU‑based retrieval.
- **Takeaway**: Matryoshka Hypencoder demonstrates that a single HyperNetwork can generate a spectrum of Q‑Net widths, achieving comparable or superior retrieval effectiveness with far fewer parameters and lower latency. The nested‑parameter approach provides flexible trade‑offs, preserves out‑of‑domain generalization, and allows efficient fine‑tuning of only the hyperhead, making dense retrieval scalable and practical for resource‑constrained environments.

#### Abstract
> The Hypencoder is a recently-proposed retrieval approach that encodes queries as shallow neural networks ("Q-Nets") that estimate relevance over pre-computed document embeddings. Inspired by Matryoshka Representation Learning, we show that the Hypencoder can be extended to support multiple sizes of Q-Nets, allowing trade-offs between effectiveness and efficiency when deployed. We find that this "Matryoshka Hypencoder" achieves comparable in-domain effectiveness with approximately 7x fewer active parameters in-domain and half as many active parameters out-of-domain, which corresponds to a 1.6-3.4x increase in scoring throughput. This work paves the way for practical deployment of Hypencoders.

#### Abstract
