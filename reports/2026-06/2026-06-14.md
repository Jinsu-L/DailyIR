### 1. ADORE: Iterative Query Expansion with Retrieval-Grounded Relevance Feedback

- **LLM Score**: 9

- **URL**: <http://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13905v1>

- **Topic Keywords**: retriever, query, relevance, relevance feedback, rag, retrieval, trec
- **Reason**: This paper is highly relevant to your research interests in Information Retrieval, particularly query understanding and ranking models, as it introduces a novel iterative query expansion framework (ADORE) that leverages retrieval outcomes for feedback. The paper's focus on retrieval-grounded relevance feedback and its evaluation on various benchmark datasets aligns well with your interests in real-time relevance optimization and deep semantic understanding.

#### T.A.R.G.E.T. Summary (from Full Text)
- **Topic**: ADORE: An Iterative, Corpus‑Grounded Query‑Reformulation System
- **Aim**: To iteratively refine a user query into a more effective one by using the retrieval outcome as explicit feedback, thereby improving retrieval quality across diverse domains.
- **Rationale**: Existing LLM‑driven reformulation methods generate expansions without inspecting the induced ranking, leading to unverified iterations and potential drift. A feedback loop that evaluates retrieved documents against the original query can guide the reformulator to reinforce relevant terms, cover missing facets, and suppress irrelevant ones.
- **Ground**: ADORE follows an Adapt‑Observe‑Evaluate cycle: (1) Adapt – generate pseudo‑passages conditioned on prior graded relevance feedback; (2) Observe – issue the expanded query to a retriever and obtain a ranked list; (3) Evaluate – assign graded relevance scores (0–3) to retrieved docs using an LLM assessor, anchoring assessment to the original query. The feedback pool is updated each round, and the loop terminates when quality, coverage, or budget thresholds are met. Prompt design shifts from zero‑shot to feedback‑conditioned across rounds.
- **Experiment**: Evaluated on passage retrieval (TREC DL19/20, DL‑Hard, MS MARCO), cross‑domain retrieval (BEIR: SciFact, TREC‑COVID, FiQA, DBPedia‑Entity, TREC‑NEWS), and reasoning‑intensive retrieval (BRIGHT: Biology, Earth Science, Economics, Psychology, Robotics, StackOverflow, Sustainable Living). Metrics: nDCG@10. Baselines include BM25, BM25+RM3, dense DPR, ANCE, Contriever‑FT, BGE‑base‑en‑v1.5, and GenQR. ADORE achieved the highest average nDCG@10 (0.555) across all settings, outperforming both sparse and dense baselines.
- **Takeaway**: Incorporating retrieval outcomes as structured feedback enables LLMs to produce more grounded, effective query reformulations. The iterative Adapt‑Observe‑Evaluate framework consistently improves retrieval performance across standard, cross‑domain, and reasoning‑intensive tasks, highlighting the value of corpus‑grounded, feedback‑driven query expansion.

#### Abstract
> LLM-based query expansion improves retrieval by enriching the original query with additional context. Yet most methods remain generation-driven, producing plausible pseudo-documents or expansions without checking how the target corpus responds. This can introduce retrieval drift, amplify misleading vocabulary, or miss terms that distinguish relevant from non-relevant documents. We argue that effective expansion requires retrieval-grounded feedback, not just single-pass generation or unverified iteration. We introduce ADORE (ADapt, Observe, Relevance Evaluate), an iterative framework that turns retrieval outcomes into feedback for the next expansion. At each round, an LLM generates pseudo-passages, a retriever exposes the corpus response, and a relevance assessor evaluates retrieved documents against the original query. These judgments identify what to reinforce, what remains undercovered, and what to suppress. Across TREC Deep Learning, BEIR, and BRIGHT, ADORE consistently outperforms strong query expansion baselines with notable improvements across nearly all evaluation settings, improving average nDCG@10 by 24.5% over BM25 and 3.6% over the strongest prior query expansion method on BEIR, and by 122.9% over BM25 and 9.2% over the best query expansion baseline on BRIGHT. Our code and data are publicly available.

#### T.A.R.G.E.T. Summary (from Full Text)
- **Topic**: CoRe – Context‑Relevance: 7B LLM query rewriter for short‑video search
- **Aim**: Train a 7B‑parameter LLM to rewrite after‑view queries into context‑aware queries that maximize the production multimodal relevance score for documents the user will engage with, using a reward identical to the live ranking fusion.
- **Rationale**: Leverage rich click/skip signals from the session as supervision; construct a monotonic, multiplicatively‑structured reward from weighted relevance scores of positives, hard negatives, and random negatives; apply safety filtering on top positives; impose length and stop penalties to control rewrite length; align reward with production fusion algebra to eliminate simulation‑production mismatch.
- **Ground**: Data: 4‑week aggregation (~2.4M instances) with strict‑click positives, no‑action hard negatives, and 5 random easy negatives per query. Reward: weighted scores S_pos, S_neg, S_rand; base reward r_base using clipped ratio with hyper‑parameters γ, α_R, R_#; safety filter removes top 2% of positives; length penalty via L_target, λ_L, β_cut. Hyperparameters: relevance weights (0.33 text, 0.67 multimodal), D_rand=5, easy‑negatives N=30, rollout max 30 tokens, γ=0.1, α_R=0.05, length target 25, λ_L=0.05, β_cut=0.98, pair k=2, β=0.1, 4 phases, cosine warm‑restart LR.
- **Experiment**: Semi‑online MPO training with DPO‑style pairwise objective: only top‑k/bottom‑k rewrites per rollout used for gradients. Mixed‑MPO loss combines BCO, SFT, MPO with weights (1.0,0.1,0.1) and β=0.1. Phase‑bounded rollout schedule amortises parameter sync; reward calls batched off‑peak. Learning‑rate warm‑restart per phase. Evaluation on production relevance score and safety metrics.
- **Takeaway**: CoRe demonstrates that a continuously fine‑tuned 7B LLM can rewrite short‑video search queries in production, achieving higher relevance scores while maintaining safety and efficiency. #### Abstract
> LLM-based query rewriters in production face a tension: the training reward must reflect how the rewrite is consumed by the production ranker, yet the training procedure must be cheap enough to support continuous redeployment as data drifts. We present CoRe (Context Relevance), such a system, redeployed weekly for over five months in a major short-video search engine. Our reward uses the deployed multimodal relevance model as its source and a multiplicative ratio form mirroring the production fusion algebra, closing the simulation-production gap that offline reward proxies leave open. A semi-online Mixed Preference Optimization loop makes this reward affordable at multi-million-instance weekly scale: a DPO-style pairwise objective restricts the gradient pass to a small top-k/bottom-k subset of sampled trajectories, and a phase structure reduces trainer/inference-server parameter syncs from per-step to per-phase. An automated promotion gate over reward-like and stability metrics detected and recovered from a real reward-hacking incident in production. Rewriter output is consumed as parallel relevance signals at recall, rawrank, and finerank without displacing the original signals, bounding rewriter-failure blast radius. Online A/B from two sequential production launches, first deploying the rewriter at finerank, then extending consumption to recall and rawrank, delivers statistically significant reductions in change-query rate on rewrite-impacted queries, with all headline relevance and engagement metrics moving in the expected direction.

#### Abstract
